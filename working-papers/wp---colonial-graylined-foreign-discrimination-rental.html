Colonial, graylined and foreign discrimination in rental housing

Citation (preprint): Bao, H. X. H. (2026). Colonial, graylined and foreign discrimination in rental housing: meta-regression evidence from fifty-four correspondence tests. Working paper (JUE submission, 16 May 2026). Status: Working paper, single-authored, JUE submission

One-line summary

Proposes a tripartite classification of housing-discrimination targets (colonial / graylined / foreign), anchors each category in a distinct mixture of Becker / Phelps-Arrow / Akerlof-Kranton mechanisms, and provides a meta-regression identification test on 54 correspondence-test studies across 22 countries showing that each category responds to its theoretically-paired country-level fractionalisation moderator.

Abstract

Correspondence-test studies of housing discrimination have accumulated steadily over the past two decades and consistently document that minority-named, immigrant-named or religiously-marked applicants receive fewer responses to rental inquiries than otherwise-comparable majority applicants. The economic theory of discrimination distinguishes three canonical mechanisms underlying this pattern: taste-based (Becker, 1957), statistical (Phelps, 1972; Arrow, 1973) and identity-driven (Akerlof and Kranton, 2000). We propose that the published correspondence-test record can be organised into three analytical categories of discrimination targets — colonial, graylined and foreign — each theoretically anchored in a different mixture of the three economic mechanisms. We extend the cross-study sample to fifty-four correspondence-test studies across twenty-two countries with fieldwork between 2003 and 2022 and provide a meta-regression identification test of the three-category reading. Each category responds to its theoretically-paired country-level diversity index: the colonial-by-ethnic-fractionalisation, graylined-by-religious-fractionalisation, and foreign-by-linguistic-fractionalisation interactions are each positive, statistically significant, and consistent in sign with the underlying mechanism. The pattern is robust to a FAT-PET correction for publication selection. Recent fieldwork from Singapore, Kuala Lumpur and Malaysia is consistent with the identity-economics reading and shows that the same identity can carry the discriminator role in one jurisdiction and the target role in another.

Key contribution(s)

  • Tripartite typology of housing-discrimination targets — colonial / graylined / foreign — each grounded in a distinct mixture of the three economic mechanisms (taste, statistical, identity)
  • Identification test via theoretically-predicted moderators: each category responds to its paired country-level fractionalisation index (ethnic / religious / linguistic)
  • 54-study meta-regression with FAT-PET correction for publication selection
  • Discriminator-role / target-role reversibility: the same identity (e.g., Malay-Muslim) is a target in non-Muslim-majority countries and a discriminator in Malay-Muslim-majority Malaysia. Strong evidence for the identity-economics reading.
  • Methodological diagnostic: estimates of any single category require simultaneous inclusion of the other two; studies with only majority + colonial profiles absorb foreign and graylined components into the colonial estimate.

Method / data

  • 54 unique correspondence-test studies, 22 countries, fieldwork 2003-2022
  • Updated sample beyond 2024 analysis (added 16 studies, +2 countries: Austria, Turkey)
  • Meta-regression: study-level odds ratios on tripartite indicators (foreign omitted as baseline), gender, year, ethnic / religious / linguistic fractionalisation, major-city indicator, country population, former-colonial-power host indicator, sample size, within-study design indicator, continent fixed effects
  • Standard errors clustered at paper level
  • FAT-PET correction for publication selection (robustness)
  • Three illustrative recent studies referenced but not in regression sample: Singapore (Bao & Tan), Kuala Lumpur (Bao et al), Wang et al 2026 Malaysia

Preliminary findings

  • Saturated specification: colonial coefficient ≈ −0.10 vs foreign baseline; graylined ≈ −0.06; male-applicant ≈ −0.11
  • Three predicted moderator interactions (colonial × ethnic-fractionalisation, graylined × religious-fractionalisation, foreign × linguistic-fractionalisation) all positive, statistically significant
  • Robust to FAT-PET correction
  • 2024 to 2026 update: smaller magnitudes than 2024 sample — possibly reflects upward shift in colonial and graylined estimates as more recent / more legally-mature markets enter

Open issues / pushback received

Pending revisions (scope statements)

Added 2026-05-19 after a framework-scoping discussion. Two scope additions to make the paper’s contribution sharper by being explicit about what the framework does and does NOT claim.

Revision 1 — Introduction (after the tripartite categories are introduced)

Add a one-paragraph scope statement establishing what the framework covers:

The tripartite classification developed here organises discrimination on group-membership axes anchored in ethnic, religious, and citizenship categories, signalled at the inquiry stage by names, accents, telephone country codes, and similar written cues. Discrimination on other axes — disability, sexual orientation, gender identity, age, and gender — operates through different mechanism mixtures, distinct cue types (body-marked, couple-structured, behavior-disclosed rather than name-signalled), and possibly different moderators. Within LGBTQ+ discrimination specifically, gay/lesbian and trans patterns differ empirically (e.g., Abbate et al. 2024 find no discrimination against gay-male couples in Latin America but strong discrimination against trans couples) and would each require separate framework analysis. The present paper retains gender as a covariate but does not develop a fourth category to capture it; gender discrimination interacts with the three categories rather than constituting a fourth alongside them.

Revision 2 — Section 2.2 (end of the section defining the three categories)

Add a paragraph clarifying the cue-type scope of the framework:

The framework treats group-membership signals that landlords can read from names, telephone country codes, accents, or written cues at the inquiry stage. Body-marked signals (disability disclosure, age, presented gender) and behavior-disclosed signals (sexual orientation revealed via couple structure, gender-identity disclosure) operate at a different point in the screening process — typically the viewing stage rather than the inquiry stage — and follow distinct theoretical paths. The three categories developed below should not be assumed to extend to these axes without separate analysis.

Why these revisions matter

  1. Sharpens the contribution by negative claim — readers know exactly what the paper claims and what it does not, which strengthens peer-review reception.
  2. Pre-empts seminar / referee question: “What about disability / LGBTQ+ / age?” — the scope statement answers this directly.
  3. Creates citation hooks for future work: by explicitly identifying axes that don’t fit, the WP signals research opportunities to follow-on authors.
  4. Preserves the 3×3×3 elegance — three categories × three economic mechanisms × three fractionalisation moderators. Trying to absorb disability or LGBTQ+ into this would dilute the mapping; scoping them out preserves it.

Empirical notes supporting the scope decision

Sexual orientation / gender identity evidence in the broader literature (from your library):

  • Paper - Gouveia Nilsson Berggren 2020 Religiosity discrimination same-sex couples Portugal — same-sex couples face discrimination in Portugal; counterintuitive negative relationship with parish religiosity (opposite of what graylined would predict if religiosity is the moderator)
  • Paper - Hellyer 2021 Same-sex rural urban USA — null result in US rural+urban
  • Paper - Abbate et al 2024 Discrimination against gay and transgender people Latin America — gay-male NULL, trans STRONG with statistical pattern (job signal closes gap); within-LGBTQ+ heterogeneity makes single-category treatment indefensible
  • Paper - Flage 2021 Same-sex couples meta-analysis — heterogeneous meta-finding

Disability evidence:

  • Paper - Flage Le Gallo 2023 Disability discrimination French rental field experiment — disability disclosure produces large discrimination; mental disability faces largest gap. Stigma is persistent and body-anchored, not symbolic-event-triggered (so doesn’t fit graylined’s event-trigger framing).
